Part 01 of 04

The problem

Why a model cannot read words, and where simple word counting breaks down.

How a model usually sees an email

A spam classifier normally reads a vector of numbers: how many links, how many urgent words, whether the sender is unknown.

There is a limitation in that approach. Someone had to decide, by hand, to count "urgent words." The email itself is language, and that is where the meaning lives. The aim here is to let the model read the words.

the usual approach
a vector of numbers  →  [3, 7, 1, …]
the goal
the words themselves  →  ?

Security runs on text

Almost every signal a security team studies is words. A model that reads text well unlocks a large share of the available data at once.

Emails
phishing, spam, business-email compromise
URLs & domains
the addresses attackers register and send
Logs & commands
endless lines from servers and shells
Malware strings
text buried inside a malicious file

A network only does arithmetic

It multiplies and adds. It has no representation of a word. So every word must become a number. The entire challenge reduces to one statement:

turn words into numbers without losing their meaning.

The text is first split into pieces

Before a model can assign numbers, it must decide what counts as one piece. Splitting the text apart is called tokenizing, and each piece is a token.

"verify your account now"
→
verifyyouraccountnow
Sometimes smaller than a word
A rare or misspelled word is split into familiar chunks, so the model is never left without a representation. "verifying" becomes "verify" and "ing."
A brief normalization first
The text is commonly lower cased, punctuation is stripped, and filler words such as "the" are dropped, so the pieces stay consistent.

Splitting into subword pieces this way is also the first step inside today's large language models.

Think it through

Which disguises survive

An attacker disguises a word to slip past a filter. Once the text is split into subword pieces, which of these still leave recognizable chunks, and which erase the word entirely?

paypa1 p@yp@l pаypal p a y p a l cGF5cGFs

Splitting into pieces provides robustness for common cases, yet some disguises still defeat it. Those gaps are what a meaning map is built to close.

The obvious idea and why it stalls

The natural first approach gives each word its own slot in a large dictionary: a reasonable, inexpensive baseline that discards the one property that must be preserved.

one word, "verify"
[ 0, 0, 1, 0, 0, … ]
one-hot, a single slot lit
→
a whole message
[ 0, 1, 0, 2, 1, 0, … ]
bag-of-words, the slots tallied
Sparse

A vocabulary of 50,000+ words yields a long vector that is almost all zeros. Wasteful and slow.

Order-blind

Only the presence of each word is tallied, so "not safe" looks almost identical to "safe."

Meaning-blind

"verify" is slot 17, "confirm" is slot 9,000. To the model they are as unrelated as "verify" and "banana."

The words became numbers, but the meaning was discarded. The numbers must carry it.

Part 02 of 04

Embeddings

Where meaning becomes distance, and how that map is learned from raw text.

Meet the embedding

Each word is assigned a short vector of numbers, arranged so that words with similar meaning receive similar numbers. As an analogue, picture every word as a point on a map: related words sit close, unrelated words sit far apart.

Meaning becomes distance.

One word becomes a short vector of numbers

That row of numbers is called a vector. No single number carries meaning on its own; the meaning lives in the whole pattern.

verify
0.82-0.310.500.11 … 64 in all
confirm
0.79-0.280.470.09 … nearly the same
banana
-0.440.66-0.120.91 … very different

The length of the list, here 64, is the embedding's dimensions.

Choosing the number of dimensions

The length of the vector is chosen before training rather than learned. This hyperparameter trades capacity against cost.

fewer dimensions

Compact and fast, and they generalize from less data. Too few, and unrelated words are crowded together with no room to separate.

more dimensions

More room to capture nuance, at the cost of memory and computation, with greater risk of overfitting on small data. Gains taper off past a point.

in practice

Classic word embeddings run roughly 50 to 300 dimensions; powers of two such as 64, 128, and 256 are conventional. Large language models go far higher, 768 and beyond. The 64 used here is illustrative.

This count is the width of one word's vector, independent of how many layers or neurons the model built on it uses.

Meaning becomes distance

Words begin as plain IDs whose position means nothing. They settle into neighborhoods by meaning, and "verify" and "confirm" drift together.

Explore: projector.tensorflow.org[1]

Closeness is measured as the cosine of the angle \( \theta \) between two word vectors \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \): a value of 1 means the same direction, 0 unrelated.

\( \cos(\theta) = \dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\lVert \mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert \mathbf{b}\rVert} \)

Directions carry meaning too

Proximity is not the only structure. On a well trained map, the direction from one word to another is itself meaningful.

king − man + woman ≈ queen

The model is not memorizing a thesaurus. It has organized words into a space where relationships become geometry. That structure is what allows it to handle words and phrasings it has barely seen.

from the paper

GloVe (Pennington, Socher & Manning, 2014)[2] trains on global co-occurrence counts so that "the resulting representations showcase interesting linear substructures of the word vector space," the very king−man+woman geometry above.

Why that arithmetic works

Each word holds a few coordinates of meaning. Subtracting man and adding woman swaps gender while leaving royalty untouched, so the result lands on queen.

Where the numbers come from

They are not set by hand. The same gradient steps that train any network's weights learn them too.

The guiding principle is old and simple:
a word is known by the company it keeps.

Words that appear in similar surroundings are nudged toward similar locations on the map.

the fill in the blank task
"please ____ your account"

The missing word is predicted from its neighbors, across billions of sentences. The only way to succeed is to give related words related vectors. Predicting the center word from its surroundings in this way is called CBOW.

from the paper

The idea is older than deep learning. The linguist J. R. Firth (1957)[3]: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps!" Embeddings are that maxim turned into geometry.

Learning the map by predicting the neighbors

A small window slides along billions of sentences; from the middle word, the model predicts the words around it, nudging the vectors each time.

Words that keep similar company are pulled toward the same region. This prediction task is skip-gram; predicting the center word from its neighbors instead is CBOW, the two architectures word2vec introduced.

from the paper

word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013)[4]: "We propose two novel model architectures for computing continuous vector representations of words from very large data sets," skip-gram being one of them.

Inside one training step

In one training step the model guesses a word's neighbors, checks the guess, and corrects itself.

please 2.0 · account 1.5 · your 1.0 · login 0.0 · verify −1.0

\[ \begin{aligned} P(w) &= \dfrac{e^{s_w}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}} \\[10pt] P(\text{please}) &= \dfrac{e^{2.0}}{e^{2.0} + e^{1.5} + e^{1.0} + e^{0.0} + e^{-1.0}} \\[10pt] &= \dfrac{7.4}{7.4 + 4.5 + 2.7 + 1.0 + 0.4} \\[10pt] &= \dfrac{7.4}{16.0} \\[10pt] &= \textcolor{#3b56d8}{0.46} \end{aligned} \]

Repeated across billions of pairs, the map organizes itself.

Part 03 of 04

Embeddings in code

Pretrained vectors, whole messages, and the Keras that runs them.

Training need not start from scratch

Others have already done the expensive training and released the maps publicly. A map can be downloaded, reused, and adjusted slightly on local data.

word2vec
Google, the model that popularized the idea.
GloVe[5]
Stanford, 400,000 words, free to download.
fastText
Meta, robust to unusual and misspelled words.

From words to a whole email

An email is many words. Averaging their vectors into one gives a single vector a classifier can read and judge.

Words to one vector
\[ \mathbf{e} = \dfrac{\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + \mathbf{v}_n}{n} \]

the email vector \( \mathbf{e} \), the average of its \( n \) word vectors \( \mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_n \).

One vector to a verdict
\[ P(\text{phishing}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{e} + b) \]

the classifier weighs \( \mathbf{e} \) with \( \mathbf{w} \) and bias \( b \); the sigmoid \( \sigma \) gives a phishing probability.

The complete pipeline in Keras

Each line corresponds to one of the steps just described. The embedding's numbers are learned while the model trains.

vectorize = TextVectorization(max_tokens=20000)
vectorize.adapt(train_emails)     # learn the words

model = keras.Sequential([
    vectorize,                       # words -> ids
    layers.Embedding(20000, 64),       # id -> 64-num vector
    layers.GlobalAveragePooling1D(),  # average the words
    layers.Dense(1, activation="sigmoid") # phishing? 0..1
])
TextVectorization

the dictionary maker: words → IDs.

Embedding

the map: one learned vector per word.

AveragePooling

compress the email's words into one vector.

Dense + sigmoid

a single neuron producing the decision.

The Embedding table starts as random numbers. There is no separate word2vec step here: the same gradient steps that train the classifier flow back into the table, so the vectors are shaped by the phishing decision itself.

Built with TensorFlow and Keras[6], the same toolkit used in production.

Or reuse a map already built

The Embedding layer is given GloVe's numbers as its starting point instead of random ones.

# GloVe: 400k words, each already a vector
glove = load_glove("glove.6B.100d.txt")
matrix = build_matrix(vectorize.get_vocabulary(), glove)

layers.Embedding(20000, 100,
    weights=[matrix],     # start from borrowed meaning
    trainable=False)     # freeze it, or fine tune later
weights = [matrix]

begin from a map trained on billions of words.

trainable = False

freeze it when only a little data is available.

Rule of thumb

little data, freeze it. Ample data, fine tune it.

Part 04 of 04

In security and beyond

Reworded phishing, context per sentence, and the road to language models.

Why this catches reworded phishing

Attackers reword their messages to slip past filters. This is exactly where embeddings help.

trained on
"verify your account"
attacker sends
"confirm your details"
Keyword filter

different words → passes straight through.

With embeddings

"confirm" sits close to "verify," and "details" to "account," so the message lands in nearly the same place and is still flagged.

in an interview

A common question: why does an embedding filter beat a keyword filter? Because it matches meaning rather than exact words, so a paraphrase still lands near what the model already recognizes.

When attackers deliberately misspell

Rewording substitutes whole words; this alters the letters instead, to evade any list of known words.

paypal → paypa1 viagra → v1agra free → fr€e account → acc0unt
Word list or keyword filter

every unusual spelling is a new token it has never seen. Out of vocabulary, no match, it passes through.

Subword embeddings (fastText)

the vector is built from letter chunks. "paypa1" shares almost all its chunks with "paypal," so it lands beside the real word and is still flagged.

That same operation, breaking a word into subword pieces, is also the first step inside a modern language model, directly on the path to LLMs.

from the paper

fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2017)[7]: "A vector representation is associated to each character n-gram; words being represented as the sum of these representations," which is why a misspelling still lands near the real word.

One word, many meanings

So far each word receives one fixed vector. But the same word can mean very different things, and a single point on the map cannot occupy two places at once.

"the river bank"
"my bank account"

One word, two meanings. A fixed embedding assigns both sentences the identical vector for "bank," blurring them together. In security the same problem affects a word like "execute": in a payroll note and a malware report it means very different things.

A vector that depends on the sentence

Newer models read the surrounding words and give each word a different vector in each sentence. These are contextual embeddings (BERT).

"the river bank"
0.210.74-0.300.05
one "bank"
"my bank account"
0.80-0.220.410.18
a different "bank"

Reading the whole sentence at once to place each word is the seed of today's large language models, and even there, meaning becomes distance still holds.

from the paper

BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)[8] is "designed to pre-train deep bidirectional representations from unlabeled text by jointly conditioning on both left and right context in all layers."

Further reading: The Illustrated Word2Vec & Transformer[9]

The same method extends beyond words

Placing items by the company they keep works for almost anything. The general name is entity embeddings.

Domains
visited by the same infected hosts → sit close.
Users
those behaving alike cluster; an outlier stands apart.
IPs & file paths
seen in similar contexts → similar vectors.
Command sequences
a shell session read as a sentence.
security

Entity embeddings turn anomaly detection into geometry: a host or account that suddenly lands far from its usual neighborhood warrants an alert.

Think it through

What is worth mapping

Within a security setting, which text, or which entities, are worth placing on a meaning map?

email subject lines DNS queries command lines file names usernames

Consider the adversary. The map is learned from data, so an adversary can craft text to land in the "safe" neighborhood, the analogue of a wolf writing in sheep's vocabulary.

Powerful, but not infallible

Inherited bias

the map absorbs the skews in the text it learned from, a fairness risk in its own right.

New or misspelled words

no vector exists for a word never seen, and attackers misspell deliberately.

Crafted text

a known map can be manipulated to land in the "safe" neighborhood.

Drift

language and attacks change; an old map grows stale and must be refreshed.

Security remains an arms race, and embeddings are no exception.

Key terms

Token

one piece of text, a word or a subword chunk, after the text is split.

Skip-gram

the training task that learns the map: predict a word's neighbors (word2vec).

Embedding (vector)

a word's short vector of numbers, its location on the meaning map.

Dimensions

the length of that vector, for example 64 or 100 numbers.

Similarity

a closeness score: near 1 means very alike, near 0 unrelated.

Contextual embedding

a word's vector recomputed for each sentence, so "bank" differs by context (BERT).

Pretrained

a map already learned by others, downloaded and reused.

Out of vocabulary

a word the map never saw, so it holds no vector for it.

The core ideas

  1. A model cannot read words, so each word must become numbers.
  2. Plain IDs discard the meaning.
  3. An embedding places each word on a map, so meaning becomes distance.
  4. Filters then match meaning rather than exact words, the defense against paraphrase.
