Part 01 of 03

The problem

Why hand written rules fail to generalize, and what is worth measuring about an email.

The phishing problem

An email arrives. Is it phishing, or legitimate?

The signals are noisy: an unusual link, an urgent tone, an unfamiliar sender. No single signal is decisive, and the tactics shift from week to week.

The objective
an email → phishing? yes or no

Rather than specifying the rule by hand, the model learns it from many labeled examples.

Why the rules are learned, not written

Rules written by hand

A hand written rule such as if links > 5 then phishing holds briefly. As attackers change tactics, exceptions accumulate until the rule set becomes unmaintainable.

Learning from examples

Given thousands of emails already labeled phishing or legitimate, the model infers the pattern itself. As phishing evolves, new examples are supplied and the model adapts.

An everyday analogue of the same computation

On reading an email, a person weighs a few signals and arrives at a judgment.

an unusual link an urgent, pressuring tone an unfamiliar sender
→
intuition reports
probably phishing

A neuron performs the same operation, but over numbers rather than intuition.

Feature engineering is where security expertise pays off: the strongest features are the ones an attacker cannot cheaply fake. Choosing them is a threat-modeling judgment, not a math one.

Where the examples come from

The model learns from emails that people have already labeled. These public collections are standard in the research literature.

Nazario Phishing Corpus[1]

A public collection of real phishing emails, gathered over many years.

Enron email dataset[2]

A large set of genuine corporate emails, the legitimate class to learn from.

A live inbox

Each "report phishing" action produces a fresh labeled example.

Labels are essential; without them there is nothing to learn from.

Which signals about an email are worth converting into features?

The neuron

Weighting a few features into a single decision.

The idea was borrowed from the brain

The first artificial neuron was a deliberately simplified model of a biological brain cell.

In the brain
In our neuron
Branches called dendrites pick up signals from other cells
The input numbers, the features describing the email
Some connections are strong, some are weak
The weights, the contribution of each feature
The cell body adds the incoming signals together
The weighted sum, plus the bias
Past a threshold it fires a pulse, otherwise stays quiet
The activation, turning the total into an output

A biological brain contains roughly 86 billion[3] neurons. The artificial version is far simpler: it borrows the structure of the idea, not the biology.[4]

From a 1943 idea to today

Neural networks are an old idea, dismissed twice and revived twice.

1943
Neurons as math

McCulloch and Pitts publish the first formal model of a neuron: on or off, past a threshold.[4]

1958
The Perceptron

Rosenblatt builds a machine that learns to separate simple patterns. The press is captivated.[5]

1969
The first winter

A prominent critique shows that a single neuron cannot learn certain simple problems. Interest and funding collapse.[6]

1986
Backpropagation

A method for training many layers at once revives the field, the engine behind everything that follows.[7]

2012
Big data

Abundant data and fast hardware let a deep network win an image recognition contest decisively. The modern era begins.[8]

The core idea changed little. What finally caught up was data and computing power.

Meet the neuron

A neuron is an elementary decision unit. It takes a few features, weights each one, sums them, and returns a single number. A network is many such neurons connected together.

phishing scorescore == squeeze(f( features × weightsw · x ++ biasb ))

What's inside a single neuron

One phishing email flows through, from raw features to a single score.

What the weights do

Each weight functions as a volume knob on one feature. Training is the search for good settings across all of them.

large

This feature carries strong influence, pushing the output toward phishing.

near zero

This feature is effectively ignored, with negligible effect on the output.

negative

This feature pushes against phishing, as a trusted, known sender would.

One weight per feature. They begin as random values and are adjusted each time the network sees a labeled example.

in practice

Reading the trained weights is a free feature importance check: the largest weights reveal what the model relies on.

What the bias does

The bias is the neuron's baseline tendency to fire, before any feature is considered.

It is a single value added to the weighted sum: a negative bias suppresses activation, a positive one promotes it.

Low bias, slow to fire
remains inactive until the evidence accumulates
High bias, quick to fire
activates on the faintest signal
in practice

The bias is learned during training; the 0.5 cutoff is set afterward. Both govern how readily the filter labels an email.

Adding up the evidence

Each feature is multiplied by its weight, the products are summed, and the bias is added.

\[ z = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \cdots + w_n x_n + b \]
the w x part

each feature times its weight, summed across all features

plus b

the bias, the baseline added to the sum

equals z

one number, the total evidence so far

No calculus is involved, only multiplication and addition.

in practice

In practice z is never computed by hand; the framework does it. But recognizing it as \( w \cdot x + b \) is what lets one debug a model that returns nearly the same score for every email.

What an activation function is

After summation the neuron holds a single number, the total z. The activation function reshapes it into a bounded form.

One family compresses the total into the 0 to 1 range, as the curve on the right shows. Another passes strong positive signals and blocks the rest.

The choice depends on where the neuron sits in the network and the form of output required.

any number ina bounded range out

Why we need an activation

At the output, shape the result

The total z is unbounded. At the output it is compressed into a probability: one form for two classes, another for more.

In the hidden layers, introduce a bend

Without a nonlinearity, stacked neurons collapse to a single straight line. The bend lets the network fit curved, real world patterns.

a straight line versus a bend
from the paper

Cybenko (1989)[9] proved a single hidden layer with a sigmoid-like activation can approximate any continuous function to any accuracy. The bend is what makes a network universal.

The problem one neuron can't solve

A single neuron can draw only one straight line. Some patterns require more.

Consider two signals: a risky link and an urgent tone.

When they disagree (a calm tone concealing a bad link, or loud urgency with nothing behind it) the email is suspicious. When they agree, it is usually benign.

No single straight line separates the four cases, and one neuron is exactly one straight cut. A hidden layer bends the space until a line suffices.

two signals, four cases
benignsuspicious
from the paper

Minsky & Papert (1969)[6] proved a single perceptron cannot learn this exact pattern, the famous XOR problem. That limit helped trigger the first AI winter, until hidden layers and backprop answered it.

Three activations, two jobs

The location of a function in the network determines which one is used.

hidden layers
ReLU
keeps positive values, sets the rest to zero. the inexpensive bend for hidden layers.
output · binary
Sigmoid
maps two classes to a single 0 to 1 probability, as in the phishing score.
output · multiclass
Softmax
maps more than two classes to percentages that sum to 100%.

ReLU provides the bend in the hidden layers. Sigmoid and softmax shape the output, selected by the number of classes: two is binary, more is multiclass.

These three are the common choices, not the full set. Others such as tanh, Leaky ReLU, and GELU each vary the same idea of bending or compressing a number.

Why ReLU runs the hidden layers

What makes sigmoid ideal at the output is what limits it in the hidden layers.

Cheaper to train

ReLU is a single comparison against zero. Sigmoid evaluates a full S curve on every pass, and its flat tails slow learning, so ReLU trains faster and more stably.

Matched to its role

ReLU is used in the hidden layers, where only an inexpensive bend is needed. Sigmoid remains on the output neuron, where its 0 to 1 compression yields the phishing score.

\[ \mathrm{ReLU}(x) = \max(0,\, x) \]
from the paper

Glorot, Bordes & Bengio (2011)[10] found ReLU units train as well or better than sigmoid and tanh, while producing sparser, cheaper representations.

Sigmoid, the binary score

For exactly two classes, a single sigmoid neuron at the output suffices.

It compresses the total into one number between 0 and 1, interpreted as a probability. A score of 0.92 indicates roughly 92% confidence that the email is phishing.

Bounded and saturating

However large the input grows, the output approaches 1 without exceeding it, and approaches 0 in the other direction.

\[ \sigma(x) = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-x}} \]
in practice

The score is the basis for action. A cutoff, 0.5 by default, converts it into a yes or no decision. Raising it is stricter; lowering it catches more.

Adding the bends makes the curve

As one input varies from low to high, a single neuron traces one bend. A layer produces many bends, and the next layer sums them into one curve.

neuron 1one bend
+
neuron 2another bend
+
neuron 3another bend
=
the layer's curveeach bend, added up

The bias sets where each bend turns on, the weight sets its slope. A negative weight points a bend downward, so the curve can dip and rise to fit any shape.

in practice

Training repositions the bends, adjusting weights and biases until the curve fits the data.

When there are more than two answers

The filter has two classes, but many problems have more. That is the role of softmax.

Consider sorting each email into three categories. The network has one output neuron per category, and softmax converts their scores into percentages that sum to 100%.

6%
Safe
12%
Spam
82%
Phishing
the model's choice

The largest percentage is selected, and sigmoid is the two class case of softmax.

\[ \mathrm{softmax}(\text{phishing}) = \dfrac{e^{\text{phishing}}}{e^{\text{safe}} + e^{\text{spam}} + e^{\text{phishing}}} \]
Each score is exponentiated, then divided by the sum across all three, giving the 6, 12, and 82 above.

This hand off is the essence of depth: one layer's outputs are the next layer's inputs. The output neuron never sees the raw email, only the hidden layer's transformation of it.

What the hidden layers do

Each layer builds on the previous one, composing simple patterns into higher level ones.

More layers provide capacity for more complex patterns. That is the meaning of "deep" in deep learning.

But depth is not automatically beneficial. More layers are harder to train, require more data, and can memorize rather than generalize.

Key terms, in plain words

Feature

one number describing the email, such as the count of unusual links.

Neuron

weights a few features, sums them, and returns one number.

Weight

the contribution of one feature, akin to a gain control.

Bias

the neuron's baseline tendency to fire, before any feature.

Activation

reshapes the total into a bounded score and introduces a bend.

Forward pass

passing an email through the network from input to output to obtain a score.

The four core ideas

  1. An email becomes a short list of numbers, its features.
  2. A neuron weights those features, sums them, and shapes the result.
  3. A network stacks neurons, composing simple patterns into higher level ones.
  4. Passing an email through, input to output, yields one score, the forward pass.
