Part 01 of 02

Learning

How the network improves from its own errors.

Measuring how wrong a prediction is

The loss is a single number measuring how far the prediction is from the truth, analogous to a golf score. Lower is better, and learning is the process of reducing it.

Confident and correct → loss near zero
Confident and wrong → loss is large
For the curious

One common scoring rule: when an email is genuinely phishing, the penalty is small if the predicted score was close to 1, and grows quickly as the score drops toward 0.

This rule is named cross entropy, and it is fundamentally a measure of surprise. A confident, correct prediction is barely surprised and pays almost nothing. A confident, wrong prediction is heavily surprised and pays a great deal. Binary denotes two classes, the partner to the sigmoid score.

With true label \( y \) in \( \{0, 1\} \) and predicted score \( p \), it reads \( \text{loss} = -[\, y \log p + (1 - y) \log(1 - p) \,] \).

Learning as a descent downhill

Consider the analogue of descending a foggy hill toward the lowest valley. Long range visibility is absent, but the local slope of the ground is felt, and a step is taken downhill. Repeated, the steps reach the bottom.

The hill is the loss. Each setting of the weights is a position on it, and the height is how wrong the network is. The objective is the lowest valley.

loss as a hill

Gradient descent, step by step

Gradient is the technical term for slope, and descent means moving down it.

Measure the slope

the direction of steepest increase, and its steepness at this position.

Step downhill

each weight is nudged slightly in the opposite direction.

Repeat

the steps continue until the loss stops decreasing.

from the paper

Stepping downhill on noisy, sampled data is stochastic approximation, introduced by Robbins & Monro (1951)[1]. Every optimizer in deep learning is a descendant of that idea.

How one weight is updated

Stated plainly, the new weight is the old weight minus a small step in the downhill direction.

new weight = old weight − small step × slope
\[ w \leftarrow w - \eta \, \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w} \]

The same statement in symbols: the weight \( w \), the learning rate \( \eta \), and the slope of the loss \( L \) for that weight.

the minus

moves opposite to the slope, the downhill direction.

small step

the distance moved each time, the learning rate, covered next.

slope

the downhill direction for this one weight.

How large a step to take

The step size is named the learning rate, and setting it correctly is what makes or breaks training.

Tiny steps

convergence is reached eventually, but painfully slowly.

Just right

a steady pace glides down into the valley.

Giant steps

the bottom is overshot and the path bounces around, or diverges entirely.

from the paper

The step size is almost never chosen by hand. Adam sets it automatically, giving every weight its own adaptive step. Kingma & Ba (2015)[2] describe "an algorithm for first-order gradient-based optimization... based on adaptive estimates of lower-order moments," the running mean and variance of the gradient. The sensible default optimizer to this day.

Practical techniques for tuning steps and inputs are collected in LeCun, Bottou, Orr & Müller (1998)[3], a standard reference.

Backpropagation: sharing the blame

Gradient descent requires the slope for every weight, and there are thousands. Backpropagation computes them all at once. Analogous to a coach reviewing a loss, it traces the error backward and assigns each weight its fair share of the blame, so each one knows how to improve.

from the paper

The method that revived the field: Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams (1986)[4] describe a procedure that repeatedly adjusts the weights so as to minimize the difference between the network's actual output and the desired output. That one trick is what makes deep networks trainable.

Epochs and batches

The analogue is studying from a stack of flashcards.

batch
a handful of cards

A small group of emails, say 32, is processed before the weights are adjusted. This is steadier and faster than one at a time.

epoch
one full pass

One pass through the entire stack. Setting epochs = 20 specifies twenty passes.

Training, validation, and test data

The network is never graded on the emails it learned from, so the labeled data is split into three parts.

This is why fit(...) sets some data aside. It keeps the grade honest.

in an interview

A classic question: why a separate test set? Because tuning on it leaks the answer. The test set provides the single honest estimate of behavior on mail the filter has never seen.

Building and training the network

Writing the network, training it, and establishing when to trust it.

Writing the network in Keras

import tensorflow as tf
from tensorflow import keras

model = keras.Sequential([
  keras.layers.Dense(16, activation="relu",
              input_shape=(20,)),
  keras.layers.Dense(8,  activation="relu"),
  keras.layers.Dense(1,  activation="sigmoid"),
])
Sequential

a simple stack of layers, front to back.

Dense(16, "relu")

16 neurons, each wired to every input.

input_shape=(20,)

20 features per email come in.

Dense(1, "sigmoid")

one output, the phishing score.

All of this runs on TensorFlow and Keras[5], the everyday tools for building and training networks like this.

Teaching the network

model.compile(
  optimizer="adam",
  loss="binary_crossentropy",
  metrics=["accuracy"],
)

history = model.fit(
  X_train, y_train,
  epochs=20,
  batch_size=32,
  validation_split=0.2,
)
optimizer="adam"

smart gradient descent that tunes the step size for each weight automatically.

loss="binary_crossentropy"

the phishing scoring rule from the loss slide, the surprise measure that punishes confident mistakes. Binary because there are two answers.

fit(...)

runs our four step loop, over and over.

validation_split=0.2

holds back 20% to check honesty on unseen mail.

Putting the filter to work

A new email arrives. Applying the trained network takes three steps, all of them already covered.

new_email = "URGENT: verify your account now"
x = email_to_features(new_email)

score = model.predict([x])[0][0]   # -> 0.92

if score >= 0.5:
    quarantine(new_email)
same features

the new email becomes numbers, exactly as before.

predict

runs the forward pass. 0.92 indicates 92% confidence of phishing.

the 0.5 cutoff

is a choice: a higher value is stricter, a lower one is more permissive.

Whether the network actually learned

loss over the rounds
training validation

As the rounds progress, the loss should fall and accuracy should rise.

The validation line, measured on emails the network never trained on, is the one to watch. It is the honest report card. If it continues to fall as well, the network is genuinely learning rather than memorizing.

Key terms, in plain words

Loss

a score for how wrong the guess was. Lower is better.

Gradient descent

stepping the weights downhill to make the loss smaller.

Learning rate

how big each downhill step is, usually set by Adam.

Backpropagation

assigning the blame backward so every weight knows how to improve.

Epoch / batch

one full pass through the data; a small group adjusted at a time.

Validation

mail held back to check honest progress during training.

The four core ideas

  1. The loss measures how wrong a prediction is.
  2. Gradient descent steps the weights downhill to reduce it.
  3. Backpropagation assigns the blame so every weight knows its part.
  4. The loop repeats over many rounds, with validation watched to keep it honest.
